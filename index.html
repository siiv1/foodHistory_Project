Dusting the Surfaces of Sudan

TASTES

Sudan has become synonyous with conflict and tragedy in the modern age. It's people have unfortunately been at the mercy of domestic and foriegn powers, who hold more regard in pillaging resources than for the well being of the commonwealth, leaving the country's rich history and untapped potential buried by the sands of time. The news headlines cover only the violence, and evoke a bad taste of empty sympathy from the world. This page looks to unearth and display the pieces of a culture cultivated by years of ancestry, influenced by exposure to many different ethnicities, and rooted in tradition and hospitality

CULTURE

Sudan social life is full of meaningful, genuine interactions all over, and keeps its people close knit even when living conditions are tough. From weekly visits to funeral processions, you can expect any friend, family, or neighbor standing with you in support. Every occassion is better shared among family or friends as hospitality holds immense weight, guests are revered and treated highly, typically with refreshments and rounds of tea or coffee, if not a full-on meal occampanied by conversations.

Humility is paramount too and you can expect gifts or offers to kindly be turned down 3 or 4 times before being taken, though it is still the responsibility of the offerer to insist until accepted. There are many unspoken rules of engagement that can be alienating at first, but exemplify sincere kindness under the surface. And that's just it, the heart of Sudan revolves around community. Perhaps it is these values that have welded together a diaspora so rich in character and resilient even in the face of oppression and violence

Motherly Charms

Long ago, Sudan and the lands around it were ruled by vast matriarchal kingdoms.

This history cascades down even today, where women uphold the backbone of the home; cooking, cleaning, preserving food, etc. Culturally, men are responsible for the finance but even outside of urban living you'll find women working agriculture or churning butter. Proudly upholding their roles as caretakers

When supplies run low, aunties are the first to pull out food they've preserved from the season. Kisra for example is a thin, fermented sorghum bread made and sold by Sudanese women and used to eat a variety of stew-type dishes called Mulahh. It's a tasty staple of Sudanese cuisine and provides numerous health benefits.

During the month of Ramadan as food is prepped for iftar(breaking fast), even with the little energy that remains from fasting all day, their kitchens are bustling; filled with laughter and conversation and flavorful aromas free flowing in and out.

There is a common phrase in arabic that exemplifies this, النساء يربطو الناس معبعد ; The women hold the people together.

Hidden Potential

Khartoum is home to the convergence of the Blue and White Niles that turn the semi arid lands of the North into lush agricultural paradises along the riversides, with some of the larges expanses of rich, fertile soil in Africa. Projects like the Gezira Scheme, designed to channel irrigation broadly, hold the potential to feed the entire population, with surplus too.

Disagreement between farmers and planners however, along with general governmental neglect, have left only a fraction of this potential realized.

Recipe: Salataat Dakwa(peanut salad)سلطة دكوة

Every Sudanese meal must come with some type of salad on the side and this is my favorite; This peanut salad is simple, tangy and deliciously nostalgic.

Serving size: 2-3

INGREDIENTS:

  • 1-2 diced tomatoes
  • 1/2 diced green pepper(optional)
  • 1/4 diced onion
  • 1/2 fresh lime
  • 1 tsp vinegar
  • 2 Tblspn sesame/olive oil
  • 2 Tblspn Unsalted natural peanut butter
  • 1 tsp paprika
  • 1 tsp salt (adjust to taste)

    • DIRECTIONS:

  • Place all vegetables together in a large bowl
  • In a smaller bowl, mix the peanut butter with the rest of the ingredients and stir for a few minutes until you get a pasty sauce
  • Combine into large bowl
  • Garnish with oil on top and serve with warm bread or as a side and Enjoy!
